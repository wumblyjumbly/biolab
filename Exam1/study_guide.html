Phylogenetics & Taxonomy

Classification

Key Concepts

  • Monophyletic (clade) — ancestral species + ALL descendants; the "gold standard" natural group
  • Paraphyletic — ancestral species + SOME descendants (excludes one or more sub-lineages)
  • Polyphyletic — group united by convergent evolution, NOT shared common ancestry
  • Clade — synonym for monophyletic group; any branch on a tree including its root and all tips
  • Taxonomic ranks (large → small) — Domain › Kingdom › Phylum › Class › Order › Family › Genus › Species
  • Arthropoda is at the rank of Phylum; Cnidaria is also a Phylum
  • Rank directly below Phylum = Class; directly above Family = Order; directly below Genus = Species

Porifera

Sponges Filter Feeders

Key Biology

  • No true tissues or organs — most basal animal phylum
  • Sessile (attached) filter feeders — suspension feeding
  • Water flow: Ostia → Spongocoel → Osculum (enter small, exit large)
  • Choanocytes drive water current with flagella and capture food particles
  • Amoebocytes transport nutrients and produce structural spicules

Cnidaria

Jellyfish, Corals, Anemones, Hydra Diploblastic

Key Biology

  • Two body forms: Polyp (sessile, upright, mouth up) and Medusa (free-swimming, bell-shaped, mouth down)
  • Three cell layers: Epidermis (outer) / Mesoglea (jelly middle) / Gastrodermis (inner)
  • Single opening = mouth AND anus (gastrovascular cavity = GVC)
  • Cnidocytes — stinging cells containing nematocysts; used for prey capture and defense
  • Classes: Anthozoa (anemones/corals, polyp only) · Hydrozoa (Obelia, both forms) · Scyphozoa (true jellyfish, medusa dominant) · Cubozoa (box jellies)

Platyhelminthes

Flatworms Acoelomate

Key Biology

  • Acoelomate — no body cavity; space between gut and body wall filled with mesenchyme (parenchyma)
  • Three classes: Turbellaria (planarians, free-living carnivores) · Trematoda (flukes, digestive parasites) · Cestoda (tapeworms, digestive endoparasites)
  • Tapeworms have no digestive system — absorb nutrients directly through tegument
  • Planarians are free-living carnivores found in freshwater, marine, and damp terrestrial environments
  • Flukes and tapeworms are digestive system parasites

Nematoda

Roundworms — Ascaris Pseudocoelomate

Key Biology

  • Pseudocoelomate — false body cavity (not fully lined by mesoderm)
  • Non-segmented, cylindrical body enclosed in cuticle (shed during molting)
  • Strong sexual dimorphism: females are larger (coiled tail) — males are smaller (curved tail with spicules)
  • Ascaris lumbricoides — large intestinal roundworm; common lab specimen
  • Complete digestive system: mouth → pharynx → intestine → anus

Annelida

Segmented Worms Coelomate · Segmented

Key Biology

  • True coelom (body cavity fully lined by mesoderm)
  • Body divided into metameres (segments) — each with its own coelom, nerves, muscles
  • Classes: Oligochaeta (earthworms, few setae) · Polychaeta (marine worms, many setae, parapodia) · Hirudinea (leeches, no setae)
  • Setae (chaetae) — chitinous bristles for locomotion in oligochaetes and polychaetes
  • Closed circulatory system; 5 pairs of aortic arches (hearts) pump blood
  • Earthworms are deposit feeders — ingest soil organic matter

Mollusca

Snails, Squid, Clams, Chitons Coelomate · Lophotrochozoa

Key Biology

  • Body plan: Mantle (secretes shell) + Foot (locomotion) + Visceral Mass (organs)
  • Classes: Gastropoda (snails/slugs) · Cephalopoda (squid, octopus, cuttlefish) · Bivalvia (clams, oysters) · Polyplacophora (chitons)
  • Cephalopods have 8 arms + 2 tentacles (squid/cuttlefish); octopus = 8 arms only
  • Siphon — jet propulsion in cephalopods; water entry/exit in bivalves
  • Radula — rasping tongue-like structure for feeding (absent in bivalves)
  • Mantle cavity — space enclosed by mantle; contains gills (ctenidia)

Arthropoda

Crayfish, Insects, Arachnids Ecdysozoa · Jointed Legs

Key Biology

  • Largest animal phylum by species count
  • Exoskeleton of chitin — shed (molted) via ecdysis for growth
  • Jointed, paired appendages; segmented body
  • Classes: Insecta (6 legs, 3 body regions) · Malacostraca (crustaceans: crayfish, crabs) · Arachnida (8 legs: spiders, scorpions) · Myriapoda (centipedes, millipedes)
  • Crayfish body = Cephalothorax (fused head + thorax) + Abdomen
  • Open circulatory system (hemolymph in sinuses) — exception to most body cavity fluid rules

Embryology

Sea Star Gastrulation · Chick Embryo Developmental Stages

Key Concepts

  • Developmental sequence: Unfertilized Egg → Fertilized Egg → Blastula → Gastrula → Early Neurula → Late Neurula
  • Blastula — hollow ball of cells; internal cavity = blastocoel
  • Gastrula — formed by invagination (gastrulation); establishes the three germ layers
  • Blastopore — opening created by invagination during gastrulation
  • Gastrocoel (Archenteron) — new internal cavity formed by invagination; becomes future gut
  • Three germ layers: Ectoderm (outer) · Mesoderm (middle) · Endoderm (inner)
  • Somites — blocks of paraxial mesoderm in chick and frog embryos; give rise to vertebrae, muscles, dermis
  • Neural plate — flat ectoderm thickening in early neurula; folds to form neural tube
  • Neural tube — hollow ectodermal tube in late neurula; becomes brain (anterior) + spinal cord (posterior)
  • Notochord — mesodermal rod beneath neural tube; induces neurulation; replaced by vertebral column in adults

Histology

Animal Tissue Types 4 Major Tissues

Four Major Tissue Types

  • Epithelial — covers surfaces, lines cavities; cells tightly packed; avascular
  • Connective — supports, binds, and fills; cells in abundant extracellular matrix (ECM)
  • Muscle — contractile; produces movement
  • Nervous — transmits electrical signals; coordinates responses
Epithelial Tissue
Simple Squamous Epithelium
Single layer of flat (scale-like) cells. Lines blood vessels (endothelium) and alveoli. Function: rapid diffusion of gases and filtration.
Simple Cuboidal Epithelium
Single layer of cube-shaped cells. Lines kidney tubules and pancreatic ducts. Function: secretion and absorption.
Simple Columnar Epithelium
Single layer of tall column-shaped cells. Lines trachea and intestines (jejunum). Function: secretion, absorption, mucus production.  ·  Goblet cells = scattered mucus-secreting cells interspersed among columnar cells (flask/goblet-shaped, pale cytoplasm)  ·  Basal lamina = thin acellular layer anchoring epithelium to connective tissue below
Stratified Squamous Epithelium
Multiple layers; surface cells flat and squamous. Forms epidermis of skin, mouth lining, esophagus. Function: protection against abrasion.  ·  Basal lamina = visible as a thin pale line at the base of the epithelial layer (interface with connective tissue)
Connective Tissue
Loose Areolar Connective Tissue
A = fibroblast (produces fibers)  ·  B = elastin fiber (yellow, branching)  ·  C = collagen fibers (thick, wavy, pink bundles). Widely spaced fibers, abundant ground substance.
Dense connective tissue — found at outer edge of tracheal cartilage on lab slide
Dense Connective Tissue
Fibroblasts = cells present (fewer than in loose CT)  ·  Collagen fibers = tightly packed, parallel bundles — much denser than areolar CT  ·  No elastin; very little ground substance. Location on trachea slide: outer rim, wrapping cartilage ring. Forms tendons and ligaments.
Hyaline Cartilage
Cells = chondrocytes (in lacunae — small spaces within glassy ECM). Strong, flexible. Covers joint surfaces, forms tracheal rings.
Bone (Osseous Tissue)
Cells = osteocytes (in lacunae within calcified ECM). Haversian systems visible. Function: structural support, mineral storage, protection.
Blood (Fluid Connective Tissue)
A = leukocyte / white blood cell (large, nucleated — immune defense)  ·  B = erythrocytes / red blood cells (small, biconcave, anucleate — O₂ transport)
Adipose Tissue
Loose connective tissue dominated by adipocytes (fat cells — large, cytoplasm nearly all lipid droplet, nucleus pushed to periphery). Function: energy storage, insulation, cushioning.
Muscle Tissue
Skeletal Muscle (Voluntary)
Long, cylindrical cells; multinucleated; visible striations (cross-banding). Attached to skeleton via tendons. Voluntary control.
Smooth Muscle (Involuntary)
Spindle-shaped cells; single central nucleus; no striations. Lines walls of blood vessels, digestive tract, uterus. Involuntary control.
Cardiac Muscle (Involuntary)
Branched cells; single nucleus; striations present; connected by intercalated discs. Heart wall only. Involuntary; rhythmic contraction.
Nervous Tissue
Nervous Tissue
A = neuron (large cell body with branching dendrites and long axon — transmit electrical signals)  ·  B = glial cell (neuroglia — smaller; support, protect, and nourish neurons)
